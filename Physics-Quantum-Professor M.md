### State space vs Euclidean space

Euclidean space is a geometric concept of flat space described by real-valued coordinates and standard distances, while **state space is an abstract, multi-dimensional vector space used to represent the condition of a dynamic system at any given moment**. 

**State space** is often a **complex vector space with potentially infinite dimensions**, and its "points" are vectors that capture the system's variables, unlike Euclidean space, where points have fixed, real coordinates.

# The QM Postulates

[Postulate 1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hJoWM9jf0gU&t=119s):  
The state of any physical system is characterised by a state vector in a **complex** vector space, which will be called the **state space of the system**.

[Postulate 2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pNFna7zZbgE):  
A physical property A of a system is described by an **operator A acting on the state space,** and **this operator is observable**.

[Postulate 3](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=p1zg-c1nvwQ):  
The result of a **measurement** of a physical quantity is one of the **eigenvalues** of the associated observable (operator), and the state of the system after the measurement is the corresponding **eigenstate** of the operator.

[Postulate 4](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u1R3kRWh1ek):  
The measurement of A (a physical quantity of the system) **in a normalised state, psi** gives eigenvalue lambda-n with probability: 

**P( lambda-n ) \= | \< Un | psi \> |^2**

Where **Un** is the nth eigenstate of the measurement operator for the physical property A.
